About Me

The Long Story (AKA: How did I get here?)

Hello, and welcome to my portfolio. My name is Josh, and as it says on the main page, I'm currently a master's candidate in the MSSD program at Boston University. However, I am probably not what you picture when you imagine a STEM student. I spent the first fifteen or so years of my career doing tradeswork - residential construction and facilities maintenance mostly, but I've done a little bit of everything in and around a job site.This was not my dream career.

In truth, I've pretty much always known that I wanted to be a software engineer, but life, as it frequently does, had other plans, and I did what I needed to to take care of my family. A few years ago I decided to go back to school to try to make that dream a reality. My academic career to that point was unremarkable, so a proper computer science program was out of the question. Still, I believed I could get there, even if I had to take the roundabout way to do it.

After three years of hard work, I completed a Bachelor of Multidisciplinary Studies degree at West Virginia University, graduating with a 4.0 GPA. My final year, I taught myself to code in Python and built a small portfolio of projects. Soon after, I was accepted into The University of Pennsylvania's Master of Computer and Information Technology program. Touted as a CS program for those without a CS background, MCIT was my first real introduction to the world of programming and computer science.

Once again, life had other plans.

A few weeks into my first semester at UPenn, my two-year-old son was diagnosed with acute lymphoblastic leukemia, or, ALL, for short. No one ever wants to hear a cancer diagnosis, but we were fortunate: ALL is extremely treatable and has a very high survival rate. It did, however, mean a nearly three-year treatment period, consisting of weekly (and sometimes more than weekly) trips to the oncologist for chemotherapy treatments and all the other fun things that accompany cancer treatment like hair loss, daily vomiting, and at-home injections. So, I took a formal leave of absence from school, and taking care of my son became my full-time job.

Six months into my son's treatment, I returned to school part-time, splitting my time between caring for him and trying to keep up with UPenn's demanding expectations. I did that for two years, and made it through the core CS classes, maintaining a 3.72 GPA. But I was stressed to the breaking point. After a second leave of absence due to my son being hospitalized for a bacterial infection, I knew that didn't want to return to UPenn. I wanted more practical work, and less theory, to be challenged without feeling like I wanted to jump off a bridge around mid-terms. So I began looking at software development/engineering degrees instead.

"Perseverance is the steadfast pursuit of an objective despite obstacles, difficulties or discouragement." - Albert Schweitzer

A few months ago, my son completed his treatment, and I transferred to Boston University into the MSSD program. Aside from BU's excellent reputation, I was impressed by their curriculum - classes in web development, mobile applications, agile methodology. All things that would be invaluable to a career-changer like myself. Although this is my first semester, I feel very strongly that I made a great choice with BU. I know that the skills I'm learning will have immediate transfer, letting me hit the ground running when I find my first position. And while the University of Pennsylvania is obviously a great school, I know that it wasn't right for me at this time in my life.

Although I know that I have a lot to learn, and that the learning will never truly stop, I believe that I have the skills, drive, and curiosity to be a great software engineer. If you'd like to join me, please check out my blog, where I plan to document the journey from maintenance man to software engineer, or the projects section where I highlight what I've built or am currently working on.

Anyway, all that was quite serious. Let's lighten the mood a bit.
